01

Anatomical Terminology

Anatomical position: Palms forward (supinated). This is the reference for ALL directional terms.

  • Superior/Inferior (cranial/caudal) — above/below
  • Anterior/Posterior (ventral/dorsal) — front/back
  • Medial/Lateral — toward/away from midline
  • Proximal/Distal — toward/away from origin
  • Superficial/Deep — toward/away from surface
  • Ipsilateral — same side | Contralateral — opposite side
  • Plantar = bottom of foot | Palmar = palm side of hand

Body planes: Median (sagittal) = flexion/extension; Frontal (coronal) = abduction/adduction; Transverse = rotation

Flexion is forward EXCEPT for the knee. Abduction of thumb breaks the rules (occurs in sagittal plane).
02

Skeletal System Overview

Axial skeleton = skull + cervical vertebrae + ribs + sternum + thoracic/lumbar vertebrae + sacrum (your midline skeleton)

Appendicular skeleton = limbs + pectoral girdle (scapula, clavicle) + pelvic girdle

Functions of bone:

  • Protection of vital structures
  • Support for body and cavities
  • Mechanical basis for movement
  • Mineral storage (calcium + phosphorus)
  • Blood cell production (medullary cavity)

Periosteum surrounds bone. Perichondrium surrounds cartilage (except articular cartilage).

03

Bone & Cartilage Types

Bone types:

  • Compact — dense, outer layer
  • Spongy/Trabecular/Cancellous — has spaces, lightweight, where blood cells form

Bone classifications:

  • Long — tubular (humerus, phalanges)
  • Short — cuboidal — ankle (tarsus) & wrist (carpus) ONLY
  • Flat — protective (cranium, sternum, ribs)
  • Irregular — face, vertebrae
  • Sesamoid — within tendon (patella)

Cartilage: Avascular (no blood supply), semi-rigid, resilient. Articular cartilage lines synovial joints. More present in younger people.

05

Bone Development

  • Diaphysis = shaft of bone
  • Epiphysis = ends of bone
  • Metaphysis = flared region between
  • Epiphyseal plate (growth plate) = lays down new bone; when it seals, you stop growing
Fracture through the epiphyseal plate in a child can affect growth!

Ossification types (POST-MIDTERM): Intramembranous (flat bones, clavicle, mandible) vs. Endochondral (mesenchyme → cartilage → bone)

06

Classification of Joints

FIBROUS JOINTS (no movement → connected by fibrous tissue):

  • Suture — between skull bones (like stitches)
  • Gomphosis — peg in socket = teeth ("sounds like gum")
  • Syndesmosis — between ulna & radius (ligament)

CARTILAGINOUS JOINTS:

  • Synchondrosis (Primary) — hyaline cartilage (epiphyseal growth)
  • Symphysis (Secondary) — fibrocartilaginous (pubic symphysis, intervertebral discs)

SYNOVIAL JOINTS (most common, free movement):

Joint cavity filled with synovial fluid. Form most joints of limbs (knuckles, hips, elbows). Lined with articular (hyaline) cartilage.

08

Thoracic Cage

  • 12 pairs of ribs + 12 thoracic vertebrae + sternum + costal cartilages
  • True ribs (1–7): attach directly to sternum via own cartilage
  • False ribs (8–10): attach indirectly via rib 7's cartilage
  • Floating ribs (11–12): end in abdominal musculature, no sternal connection

Sternum: Manubrium (top, T3–T4 level) + Body + Xiphoid process

Sternal angle (of Louis) = junction of manubrium + body, at level of 2nd costal cartilage & T4–T5 disc. It is a SYMPHYSIS.

Jugular notch = suprasternal notch (superior border of manubrium)

Ribs 11 & 12 have no neck or tubercle (no costotransverse joint). Ribs 1, 10, 11, 12 have only ONE facet on head.
09

Vertebral Column

33 vertebrae total: 7 cervical + 12 thoracic + 5 lumbar + 5 sacral (fused) + 4 coccygeal (fused)

Curvatures:

  • Primary (kyphotic) = thoracic + sacrum (present from birth)
  • Secondary (lordotic) = cervical + lumbar (develop after birth)
  • Kyphosis = humpback | Lordosis = sway back | Scoliosis = lateral S-shaped shift

Vertebral structure: Body + Vertebral arch (pedicles + laminae) + Spinous process + Transverse processes + Articular processes (2 superior, 2 inferior)

Intervertebral foramen = where spinal nerves exit. Facet joints (zygapophyseal) = plane synovial joints.

10

Vertebrae by Region

Cervical (C3–C7): Small body with uncinate processes, transverse foramen (C1–C6 for vertebral arteries), bifid spinous process. C7 = vertebra prominens.

C1 (Atlas) = ring shape, no body, has anterior/posterior arches, lateral masses. C2 (Axis) = has Dens (odontoid process) — pivot joint.

Thoracic: Has costal facets (demifacets) on body for rib articulation, transverse costal facets. Long spinous process.

Lumbar: Massive kidney-shaped body, large vertebral foramen, long/slender transverse processes, thick short spinous process.

Sacrum: Base (superior), apex (inferior), sacral promontory, foramina, hiatus (S4–S5 no lamina). Medial crest = fused spinous; intermediate = fused articular; lateral = fused transverse.

NO intervertebral disc between C1 and C2. Atlanto-occipital joint = nod "yes". Atlanto-axial = shake "no".

12

Pelvis & Pelvic Girdle

Pelvic girdle = ring of 3 bones: 2 innominate bones + sacrum

Innominate bone = ilium + ischium + pubis (fused)

  • Pelvic inlet = superior pelvic aperture
  • Pelvic outlet = inferior pelvic aperture
  • Greater (false) pelvis = above the inlet (big upper part)
  • Lesser (true) pelvis = within/below inlet
  • Ischial tuberosity = "sit bones"

Key Joints: Sacroiliac (compound: synovial plane + fibrous/syndesmosis), Pubic symphysis (secondary cartilaginous)

Greater sciatic foramen = piriformis muscle + sciatic nerve pass through

Lesser sciatic foramen = tendon of obturator internus passes through

Sacrotuberous + sacrospinous ligaments = form lesser sciatic foramen. Sacrospinous alone = forms greater sciatic foramen.
13

Muscular System Basics

Myology: Muscles can only PULL, never push. Under voluntary control.

Tendon = muscle to bone | Ligament = bone to bone (prevents unwanted movement)

Muscle roles:

  • Prime mover (Agonist) = main muscle for movement (concentric)
  • Antagonist = opposes prime mover, relaxes as it contracts (smooth movement)
  • Synergist/Helper = assists prime mover, prevents unwanted joint movement
  • Stabilizer/Fixator = steadies proximal part during distal movement

Biceps (agonist for elbow flexion) vs. Triceps (antagonist)

Structural unit = muscle fiber. Functional unit = motor unit (motor neuron + fibers it controls). Large motor units = gross movements (leg). Small = fine movements (eye).
14

Muscle Contractions

  • Reflexive = automatic (not voluntary) — knee jerk, diaphragm breathing
  • Tonic = slight sustained contraction (muscle tone) — maintains posture, no movement
  • Isometric = muscle length same, tension increases, NO movement (deltoid holding arm in abduction)
  • Isotonic = muscle changes length → produces movement

Muscle layers (deep to superficial):

  • Endomysium — around single muscle fiber
  • Perimysium — around fascicles
  • Epimysium — superficial outer layer
15

Deep Back Muscles

All innervated by posterior rami of spinal nerves. Enclosed by thoracolumbar fascia (3 layers).

Superficial layer: Splenius — runs ipsilateral

  • Splenius cervicis → cervical transverse processes
  • Splenius capitis → mastoid process of skull

Intermediate (Erector Spinae) — "I Love Spaghetti":

  • Iliocostalis | Longissimus (to mastoid) | Spinalis (to spinous processes)

Deep layer (Transversospinales):

  • Semispinalis (contralateral, bulge in neck)
  • Multifidus (contralateral) | Rotatores (very small)

Suboccipital muscles (4): Rectus capitis posterior major/minor, Obliquus capitis superior/inferior → form suboccipital triangle (contains vertebral artery + suboccipital nerve)

16

Lower Limb — Bones

Regions: Gluteal → Femoral → Knee (genus) → Leg (cruris) → Ankle (talocrural) → Foot (pedis)

Your thigh ≠ your leg! Leg = tibia + fibula region only.

  • Femur = longest/heaviest bone, articulates with tibia (NOT fibula directly)
  • Patella = sesamoid bone, formed after birth, in quadriceps tendon → becomes patellar ligament → tibial tuberosity
  • Tibia = weight-bearing (shin bone). Gerdy's tubercle = IT band attachment
  • Fibula = non-weight bearing, lateral, for muscle attachment. Syndesmosis with tibia.
  • Talus = only foot bone that articulates with leg bones (tibia + fibula). No muscles/tendons attach directly.
  • Calcaneus = heel bone
Ligament of head of femur = transmits artery ONLY (no stabilization role). Tibia is weight-bearing; fibula is NOT.
17

Hip, Knee & Ankle Joints

Hip joint: Ball & socket. Acetabular labrum (fibrocartilage). Hip ligaments all prevent hyperextension: Iliofemoral (Y-shaped), Ischiofemoral, Pubofemoral (resists abduction).

Knee joint: Modified hinge joint. Articulations: 2 femorotibial + 1 femoropatellar (NOT fibula).

  • MCL resists valgus (lateral → medial force). If MCL injured → meniscus likely injured too.
  • LCL resists varus (medial → lateral)
  • ACL = weaker, anterior tibia → posterior femur. Prevents hyperextension.
  • PCL = stronger, prevents hyperflexion. Opposite of ACL.
  • Medial meniscus = C-shaped | Lateral meniscus = circular

Ankle (Talocrural): Only hinge movement. Talus articulates w/ tibia + fibula. Dorsiflexion = more stable (talus wider anteriorly).

Lateral ligaments (limit inversion): Anterior/Posterior talofibular + Calcaneofibular

Medial (deltoid) ligament (limits eversion): Tibionavicular, Tibiocalcaneal, Tibiotalar

18

Lower Limb Muscles

Compartments of thigh:

  • Anterior (extensors) = femoral nerve
  • Medial (adductors) = obturator nerve
  • Posterior (flexors) = tibial division of sciatic nerve

Quadriceps: Rectus femoris + Vastus lateralis/medialis/intermedius → knee extension via patellar ligament

Hamstrings (sit bones → fibula/tibia): Biceps femoris (long + short head), Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus → knee flexion + hip extension

Short head of biceps femoris = only hamstring that does NOT cross hip

Pes Anserinus (medial knee): SGT = Sartorius + Gracilis + Semitendinosus

Gluteus maximus = sit-to-stand, external rotation, extends hip → inferior gluteal nerve

Gluteus medius/minimus = abduct + internally rotate hip → superior gluteal nerve

Gracilis = only adductor to cross BOTH knee and hip joints.
19

Leg & Foot Compartments

Leg compartments:

  • Anterior (Dorsiflexors) = deep fibular nerve — Tibialis anterior, Ext. digitorum longus, Ext. hallucis longus, Fibularis tertius
  • Lateral (Everters) = superficial fibular nerve — Fibularis brevis (5th metatarsal), Fibularis longus (1st metatarsal + medial cuneiform)
  • Posterior-Superficial = Gastrocnemius (crosses knee), Soleus (better plantar flexor), Plantaris → calcaneal tendon
  • Posterior-Deep (TOM DICK HARRY mnemonic) = Tibialis posterior + Flexor digitorum longus + Flexor hallucis longus

Popliteus = unlocks fully extended knee (rotation)

FHL starts laterally and ends medially; FDL = vice versa (they cross)

Foot layers (sole): Layer 1 (superficial): Abductor hallucis, Flexor digitorum brevis, Abductor digiti minimi. Layer 2: Quadratus plantae + Lumbricals. Layer 3: Flexor hallucis brevis (has sesamoid bones!), Adductor hallucis, Flexor digiti minimi brevis. Layer 4: Plantar interossei (PAD) + Dorsal interossei (DAB)

20

Spinal Cord & Nervous System

Spinal cord: Conus medullaris ends at L1/L2. Cauda equina = nerve roots below. Filum terminale = anchors cord.

31 pairs of spinal nerves: 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal

  • Afferent fibers = sensory (posterior/dorsal horn) → to brain
  • Efferent fibers = motor (anterior/ventral horn) → to muscles

Meninges (deep → superficial):

  • Pia mater (innermost)
  • Arachnoid mater (middle, spiderweb-like)
  • Dura mater (outermost, toughest)

CSF = in subarachnoid space (between pia and arachnoid). Allows brain/cord to float.

Peripheral nerve coverings: Endoneurium (single axon) → Perineurium (fascicle) → Epineurium (whole nerve)

Myelin sheath = made by oligodendrocytes (CNS) / Schwann cells (PNS). Increases signal speed.

Posterior rami = supply deep back muscles + overlying skin. Anterior rami = supply everything else.
